A practical guide to customer conversations

The Mom Test

How to talk to customers and learn if your business is a good idea when everyone is lying to you.
By Rob Fitzpatrick · Slide summary
The problem

Talking to customers is hard.

We know we ought to talk to customers. Many of us do talk to customers. But we still build stuff nobody buys.

Bad customer conversations aren't just useless. They give you a false positive that causes you to over-invest your cash, your time, and your team.
Introduction

Customer learning is archaeology.

The truth is fragile. Each blow with your shovel gets you closer, but you'll smash it to pieces if you use too blunt an instrument.

Bulldozer & dynamite

"Do you think it's a good idea?" Heavy questions that force people to flatter you.

Toothbrush

Flinching from digging deep — never finding out if anything is buried.

The truth is the goal. Questions are the tool. Learn to wield them.

Chapter 1

The Mom Test

A set of simple rules for crafting good questions that even your mom can't lie to you about.

Failing the mom test

"Mom, do you like my idea?"

A founder pitches an iPad cookbook app. Mom is ready to lie.

Son:Mom, would you ever buy an app that's like a cookbook for your iPad? Only $40.I am optimistically asking a hypothetical and you know what I want you to say.
Mom:Hmmm…As if I need another cookbook at my age.
Mom:Oh, well yes honey, that sounds amazing. $40 is a good deal.I have made a non-committal compliment and offered a feature request to appear engaged.
Son:Yes, definitely. Thanks mom — love you!I have completely mis-interpreted this conversation and taken it as validation.
Passing the mom test

Don't mention the idea. Talk about her life.

Son:Hey mom, how's that new iPad treating you?
Mom:Oh — I love it! I use it every day.
Son:What's the last thing you did on it?Get specific about examples in the past to get real, concrete data.
Mom:Your father and I are planning a trip — I was figuring out where to stay.
Son:Did you use an app for that?
Mom:No, I just used Google. I didn't know there was an app.Younger folks use the App Store as a search engine; older folks wait for recommendations.

Mom couldn't lie because we never talked about our idea.

The Mom Test

Three simple rules.

1

Talk about their life

Not your idea. The moment you reveal the idea, biases pour in.

2

Specifics in the past

Not generics or opinions about the future. People are over-optimistic about what they'll do.

3

Talk less, listen more

You don't learn anything while your mouth is moving.

Customer conversations are bad by default. It's your job to fix them.
Chapter 2

Avoiding bad data

False negatives kill ideas that could've worked. False positives kill companies. The second is worse.

Bad data comes in three flavors

What's polluting your conversations?

★

Compliments

"That's so cool — I love it!" Fool's gold of customer learning: shiny, distracting, worthless.

∿

Fluff

Generics ("I usually"), future-tense promises ("I would"), hypotheticals ("I might").

✦

Ideas

Their feature requests are pointers to motivation, not orders to build.

Fix → deflect compliments, anchor fluff, dig beneath ideas.

The deadliest fluff

"I would definitely buy that."

It sounds concrete. It's not. People are wildly optimistic about future selves. Folks are always more positive, excited and willing to pay in the imagined future than once it arrives.

Anything involving the future is an over-optimistic lie.
Anchoring fluff

Pull them out of la-la land.

Them:I'm an 'Inbox 0' zealot. It's totally changed my life.A generic (fluffy) claim.
You:Haha, nice. What's your inbox at right now?Let's get specific to see if this fluff holds up.
Them:Looks like about ten that have come in since this morning.Facts!
You:When's the last time it totally fell apart for you?
Them:Three weeks ago. I was traveling and the hotel internet didn't work — took me 10 days to recover.

Generic claim → concrete past behavior. Now you can learn.

Deflecting compliments

"That's awesome — keep me in the loop."

Symptoms in the meeting
  • "Thanks!"
  • "I'm glad you like it."
Symptoms back at the office
  • "That meeting went really well."
  • "Everybody loves the idea."
  • "We're getting positive feedback."
Compliments are the fool's gold of customer learning: shiny, distracting, and worthless.
Chapter 3

Asking important questions

A non-biasing question that doesn't move the business forward is just spinning your wheels.

Good question / bad question

Spot the difference.

BAD
"Do you think it's a good idea?"

Only the market can tell you. Everything else is opinion.

BAD
"Would you buy a product that did X?"

Hypotheticals from optimistic people who want to make you happy.

BAD
"How much would you pay for X?"

The number gives a false sense of rigor.

GOOD
"Talk me through the last time that happened."

Show, don't tell. Learn from actions, not opinions.

GOOD
"What are the implications of that?"

Distinguishes "I'll-pay-to-fix" from "annoying-but-livable" problems.

GOOD
"How are you dealing with it now?"

Reveals workflow, workarounds, and a price anchor.

Love bad news

"Meh" beats "Wow!"

Most informative responses sound like: "Umm, I'm not so sure about that" or "That's pretty neat." Both are lukewarm signals that they don't care.

The classic error is to "up your game" and pitch them until they say something nice. Unless they're holding a check, the only thing you'll gain are false positives.
There's more reliable information in a "meh" than a "Wow!" You can't build a business on a lukewarm response.
Look before you zoom

Don't drown in details before you know the big picture.

If you zoom in on a sub-problem before knowing whether they care about the area at all, every detail you collect is misleading.

  • "What are your big goals right now?" → finds priorities they actually act on.
  • "How seriously do you take your blog?" → tests whether the problem is even in their top 3.
  • "What are the 3 big things you're trying to fix right now?" → forces honest ranking.
Start broad. Don't zoom in until you've found a strong signal.
Prepare your list of 3

Always have your 3 biggest questions ready.

Pre-planning the scary questions makes them easier to face. Update the list as your beliefs change.

You should be terrified of at least one of the questions you're asking in every conversation.
Chapter 4

Keeping it casual

Strip the formality. The first conversation works better as a chat than a meeting.

No "interviews"

Just a chat — over coffee, at a meetup, in a queue.

When you strip the formality, conversations become so fast and lightweight you can leave a meetup with a dozen of them under your belt — each as valuable as a long meeting.

Learning about a customer and their problems works better as a quick, casual chat than a long, formal meeting.
Why it works
  • No 4-hour calendar dance per 1-hour meeting
  • No biasing "I'm here to interview you about my startup"
  • People love talking about themselves and their problems
  • You're more memorable and more likely to get a follow-up
Chapter 5

Commitment & advancement

Stop collecting zombie leads. Force the meeting to succeed or fail.

Meetings either succeed or fail

There's no such thing as a meeting that just "went well."

Commitment — they give up something they value (time, reputation, money).

Advancement — they move to the next step in your real funnel.

If you don't know what happens next after a product or sales meeting, the meeting was pointless.
Currencies of conversation
  • Time — next meeting with goals, wireframe review, real trial
  • Reputation — intro to peers, decision-makers, public testimonial
  • Money — letter of intent, pre-order, deposit
How did the meeting go?

What did they actually say?

BAD
"That's so cool. I love it!"

Pure compliment. Zero data.

STALL
"Looks great. Let me know when it launches."

"Don't call me, I'll call you" in disguise.

DANGER
"I would definitely buy that."

Sank one founder for $10M. Fluffy future promise.

GOOD
"What are the next steps?"

A meeting just needs to advance — even without a check.

GREAT
"Can I buy the prototype?"

The best meeting conclusion you can hear.

GREAT
"When can you talk to the rest of the team?"

B2B win — they're staking reputation.

Chapter 6

Finding conversations

Stop asking for "interviews." Find excuses to talk about a topic you both care about.

Three modes of finding people

Going to them, bringing them to you, immersing yourself.

Going to them
  • Cold outreach (low yield, occasional gold)
  • Seizing serendipity at events
  • Find a good excuse
  • Landing pages → email those who sign up
Bringing them to you
  • Organize your own meetups
  • Speaking & teaching
  • Industry blogging
  • Build helpful tools as bait
Immersing
  • Hang out where they hang out
  • Make yourself a regular
  • Become part of the community
If it's not a formal meeting, you don't need to make excuses about why you're there. Just ask about their life.
Chapter 7

Choosing your customers

Startups don't starve, they drown. Slice your segment until you know exactly who and where.

Customer slicing

From "students" to a who-where pair.

A fuzzy segment causes mixed feedback. Slice until you know who wants it most and where to find them.

  • Within this group, who wants it most?
  • Why does that sub-set want it? What's their specific motivation?
  • Does everyone in the group share that motivation, or only some?
  • Which other groups have the same motivation?
"Finance professionals, 25–35, in a major city" → worthless.
"Finance professionals in London training for a marathon, going to the gym at lunch" → reachable.
Good customer segments are a who-where pair. If you don't know where to find them, keep slicing.
Chapter 8

Running the process

Prep · keep it casual · ask good questions · push for commitment · take notes · review with team.

The full loop

Before · During · After every conversation.

Before
  • Pick a focused segment
  • Decide your big 3 learning goals
  • Decide ideal commitment
  • Do desk research first if possible
During
  • Frame the conversation
  • Keep it casual
  • Ask Mom-Test questions
  • Deflect, anchor, dig
  • Take good notes
  • Push for commitment & next steps
After
  • Review notes & quotes with team
  • Update beliefs and plans
  • Decide next 3 big questions
If you don't know what you're trying to learn, you shouldn't bother having the conversation.
If you must ask for a meeting

Frame: Vision · Framing · Weakness · Pedestal · Ask.

  1. 1

    Vision

    Half-sentence on how you're making the world better.

  2. 2

    Framing

    Where you're at and what you're looking to learn.

  3. 3

    Weakness

    Where you're stuck — show that you genuinely need help.

  4. 4

    Pedestal

    Show that they in particular can provide that help.

  5. 5

    Ask

    Ask for help. People want to give it.
