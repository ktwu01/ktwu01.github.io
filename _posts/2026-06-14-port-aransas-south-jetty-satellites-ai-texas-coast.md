[Port Aransas South Jetty](https://www.portasouthjetty.com/articles/how-satellites-and-ai-protect-the-texas-coast/) published an Institute Insights feature on May 28, 2026 about how our University of Texas team is using satellite remote sensing and AI to monitor Texas coastal water quality.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

> Source: [How satellites and AI protect the Texas coast](https://www.portasouthjetty.com/articles/how-satellites-and-ai-protect-the-texas-coast/), Port Aransas South Jetty, May 28, 2026. Byline: Mathea H. Kurtz-Shaw.

The piece connects field measurements in Port Aransas with the broader remote-sensing problem: point samples of salinity, temperature, and turbidity are accurate but sparse; satellite observations can help fill in the bay-scale picture. Our proposed direction is an AI-enabled dashboard that estimates chlorophyll and turbidity across Texas bays, from Galveston to Port Aransas, and helps make coastal water-quality signals usable for oyster farms, beach managers, conservation teams, and residents.

For me, the important part is that "AI for Earth science" becomes concrete here. It is not a generic model demo. It is satellite data, local water-quality measurements, field campaigns, and operational decisions on a coast where storms, dredging, freshwater pulses, and sediment affect real people.

The feature also includes a field photo of University of Texas system scientists in Port Aransas, including Chenxi Hu, Koutian Wu, Sharon Herzka, Chris Lowery, John Goff, Zong-Liang Yang, and Ziming Hu.
