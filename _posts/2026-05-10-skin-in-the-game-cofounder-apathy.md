The most dangerous dynamic in an early-stage startup is not conflict. It is the peace that comes from never actually committing to anything.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

We call it Consensus Theater. Founders sit in a room, vaguely agree on a bloated product roadmap, leave the meeting feeling aligned, and nobody is. Worse, nobody is even paying attention anymore.

Why? Because no one has skin in the game.

Nassim Nicholas Taleb's whole framework for skin in the game boils down to this: you only have a real stake in an outcome if you personally bear a share of the risk when things go wrong. If you only stand to gain when things go right but never explicitly suffer the consequence of being wrong, your commitment is hollow. And hollow commitment, over time, just becomes apathy.

When a product gets built through vague consensus, no one has staked their reputation on it. If the product fails to find traction, any co-founder can easily rationalize their way out: well, I didn't really think that five-module AI system was a good idea anyway. They are protected from the failure because they never explicitly committed to a specific, vulnerable vision. The escape hatch was always open.

That is the structural problem. And the cure for being scattered is not comprehensive management. It is comprehensive subtraction. And the mechanism for subtraction is forcing skin in the game.

During our Subtraction Sprint at Tacite, the mechanism we used was simple but deliberately painful. Every co-founder writes their own 25-word product definition and signs their name to it, alone, without coordination.

That breaks the Consensus Theater immediately.

When you write your solo draft, you put your intellect, your assumptions, your professional judgment on the line. If your version of the wedge gets torn apart in the alignment meeting, it stings personally. That sting is the risk. But if your wedge gets validated by real customers, you feel something intense and hard to fake: actual ownership.

And here is the thing I've come to believe pretty firmly, you cannot have true ownership over a startup without first bearing the risk of being explicitly, embarrassingly wrong about its direction. The two things come together. You cannot have one without the other.

By forcing everyone to commit their exact vision to writing before the meeting, we re-introduced skin in the game. And with skin in the game, apathy dies. Not because you scared people, but because now it's personal. It matters to them whether they are right. And that mattering is the thing you actually need from a co-founder.
