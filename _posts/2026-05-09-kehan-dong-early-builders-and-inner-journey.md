Most VCs and accelerators talk about supporting founders. Kehan Dong specifically supports the kind of founder who started building at 16, when nobody told them they were allowed to, and it turns out that population is dramatically underserved.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

Kehan describes his own work like this. "I work with people who start building earlier than they're supposed to. Early founders, unconventional paths, long-term agency." That sentence is the most honest mission statement I have read from anyone in the early-stage ecosystem in China, and it cuts at the thing most accelerators paper over, which is that the existing infrastructure is built for the conventional path, and the people who deviate from it usually have to invent the support they need.

What he actually does has three pieces. He is an angel investor in early-stage founders. He is the founder of Inner Journey, a transformational program for founders and leaders focused on conscious growth and inner alignment. And he is the founder of Code of Love, an NGO focused on social impact and educational initiatives. He is also a mentor and educator, including the lead instructor for the Startup Go Camp at Duke Kunshan University.

The Startup Go Camp is the part I want to talk about, because it is the most concrete intervention. It is a 6-week intensive entrepreneurship course at Duke Kunshan, run in partnership with the DKU Innovation and Entrepreneurship Initiative. The student rating is 90.4 out of 100. The curriculum covers the standard stack, market demand, user research, MVP development, business strategy, team management, fundraising, investor connections, but the actual differentiator is the operating model. He runs 4 hours of weekly office hours for one-on-one student interaction. He brings in classic business cases like the Facebook startup story but pairs them with personal entrepreneurial experience. There are field trips to actual technology companies. The repeated message is that all real businesses start by solving a real problem.

The reason this works is that he is not optimizing for the polished pitch deck. He is optimizing for whether the student actually starts something. The reported outcomes back this up. Many of his students continued building their projects after the course ended, not because the course gave them a credential, but because the course functioned as the launch ramp. Particularly notable is the strong participation from freshmen. He reaches the people early enough that the building habit forms before the resume-optimization habit forms.

Inner Journey is the part of his work that is harder to write about without sounding mystical. The framing is, founders need to develop inner alignment alongside outer achievement, otherwise the achievement becomes brittle. The program is described as based on ancient wisdom, holistic and measurable, with 25+ years of experience and 1000+ lives transformed. I will be honest, I am ordinarily allergic to "transformational journey" language. But the specific point that founders who only optimize the external metric eventually hit a wall, and the wall is internal, is one I have watched play out in people I know personally. The work to address that has to be done somewhere, and Kehan has built one of the somewheres.

The reason he is in this chronicle for me, sitting in Austin doing a PhD, is that I came up through the Shenzhen X-Institute fellowship, which is a sibling program in spirit to the kind of early-builder ecosystem he is operating in. The People's Daily piece on X-Institute that profiled my own path described essentially the same thing he describes. People feeling directionless inside the conventional academic track, finding their actual interests in an unconventional environment, and only then becoming productive. The systems that produce that are rare, and the people who run them are even rarer.

The career-shape lesson from his work is interesting because it inverts the usual pattern. Most successful operators eventually become passive investors, bored. He has gone the other direction. He invests, but he also actively teaches a six-week intensive at a university in Kunshan, runs a transformational program, and operates an NGO. The energy commitment is significant. The signal it sends is that the work matters more to him than the leverage.

There is one particular line from his teaching philosophy that stuck with me. "Everyone starts with ideas that might seem silly, that's normal." This is the line you wish you had heard as a 17-year-old when you were sitting on an idea you were embarrassed to say out loud. In a culture that punishes obvious dumbness early, the people who explicitly create the safety to be dumb are doing something much more valuable than the next demo day.

For me, the takeaway is, if you found yourself building something earlier than the system thought you were ready for, the right move is to find the people who already know that population exists. They are not in the obvious places. They run their own programs in their own corners, often with less branding than they deserve. Kehan's corner is one of them, and the people who pass through it tend to keep going.

The deeper bet he is making is that long-term agency is the thing that actually scales. Not capital, not network, not credentials. The person who keeps making decisions for their own reasons, year after year, is the person who eventually builds something durable. The job of the early-stage ecosystem should be to protect that agency until it can defend itself. He has built one of the more deliberate versions of that protection.
