Ego does not show up as arrogance. It shows up as a set of fake scoreboards you secretly keep, and the moment you confuse the scoreboard with the actual game, you start losing.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

Someone asked me today whether I have an ego problem. I said yes, and I want to be specific about it, because vague self-flagellation is just another flavor of ego. So here are the four concrete ego mistakes I have made or am still making as a 22-year-old founder.

### Mistake 1: Counting lines of code as a measure of self-worth

For a long time my proxy for "I am a real builder now" was commit count, lines of code, repo stars, GitHub contribution graph density. Four thousand commits in a year felt like a trophy.

It is not. It is an activity metric, and activity metrics are the easiest thing in the world to game, especially when you have AI agents in the background generating code for you. Nobody pays for commits. Users do not care how many lines of code your product is. The only thing that matters is whether the product solves a real problem for someone willing to pay.

Counting commits is the founder version of a student counting how many hours they sat at the desk. It feels like work, it photographs well, and it is a substitute for the harder question of whether anything you did today actually moved the company.

### Mistake 2: Treating fundraising as the score

The second ego trap is treating any fundraising at all as a win. The truth is the funding I was offered was small, and the term sheet was bad. Really bad. I did not sign it, which in retrospect was the only correct decision in that whole sequence.

But for a few weeks, the fact that someone offered me money at all was operating as evidence that I was a "real founder." That is backwards. Money on bad terms is not a vote of confidence in you, it is a bet that the investor can extract value from you cheaply later. A term sheet is a contract, not a compliment. If you accept a bad one because the alternative feels like admitting you are not yet fundable, you have just paid for the feeling of legitimacy with the actual ownership of your company.

The lesson: fundraising is a means, not a milestone. The moment you start posting "we raised X" instead of "we shipped Y to N customers who pay Z," you have started optimizing for the wrong scoreboard.

### Mistake 3: Building the product without understanding the business

The third mistake is the classic technical-founder failure mode. I built the product first and assumed the business would follow.

It does not. A product without a business model is a hobby with extra steps. I knew this in the abstract for a long time, the way you know a fact in a textbook, but I did not actually live it until I had a working prototype and realized I could not articulate, in one clean sentence, who the buyer was, what the contract looked like, what the price was, what the sales motion was, and what the renewal trigger was.

Tacit knowledge is a real research pain point. That does not automatically mean researchers will write a check, that PIs will approve the line item, that procurement will let the contract through, or that the deployment will survive the first IT review. Each of those is a separate problem. None of them get solved by writing more code.

### Mistake 4: Understanding the business but not moving fast enough on it

The fourth mistake is the most embarrassing, because it happens after you already know better.

Once you finally understand that business motion matters more than code motion, the natural next failure is to keep treating "thinking about the business" as the work. Drafting positioning documents. Sketching ICPs. Reading about GTM frameworks. Having long conversations with mentors about market entry. None of this is selling.

The only thing that compresses the gap between you and a real company is talking to a buyer this week, asking for money this week, and either getting a yes, a no, or a specific reason. Everything else is a higher-resolution version of procrastination. The interval between "I now understand we need to sell" and "I am actually on a call asking someone to pay" is a direct measurement of how much ego is still in your operating system, because what fills that interval is the fear of looking dumb in front of a real customer.

### What ego actually is

If I had to define ego after writing this out: ego is the gap between the scoreboard you keep in your head and the scoreboard the world actually keeps for you.

Lines of code is your scoreboard. Revenue is the world's scoreboard.

Fundraising announcements are your scoreboard. Cap table quality and runway are the world's scoreboard.

A finished product is your scoreboard. A signed contract is the world's scoreboard.

A clear understanding of the GTM motion is your scoreboard. The number of conversations you had with paying customers this week is the world's scoreboard.

Every one of those gaps is an ego tax I am paying right now. Writing them down is not the fix. The fix is closing the gap one scoreboard at a time, starting this week.
