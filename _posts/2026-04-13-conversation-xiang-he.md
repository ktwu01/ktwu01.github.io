*This is part of our "Research & Recreation" series, where we share conversations with Jackson School graduate students about their incredible work and interests beyond the classroom.*

Discipline tags: geodynamics, mantle convection, computational geophysics, plate tectonics

Sitting down with Xiang, I was immediately struck by his enthusiasm for understanding Earth's deep interior. He's a first-year PhD student working with Thorsten Becker on global mantle flow models—investigating how the deep mantle contributes to vertical signals we observe at the surface. "I'm fascinated by large-scale geodynamic processes," he tells me. "Subduction, mantle convection, continental deformation—these massive forces that shape our planet over millions of years."

## From USTC to UT Austin

Xiang's journey to Jackson School began at the University of Science and Technology of China (USTC), where he earned his B.S. in geophysics. Even as an undergraduate, he was diving into ambitious research projects. "I worked on planetary crust formation, investigating how water in the deep mantle might have influenced ancient continental crust," he explains. "We found that generating continental crust with a dry plume was almost impossible—which showed how crucial deep water was in this process."

His undergraduate thesis explored the African plume's influence on the Tethys evolution, specifically investigating why the Indian plate showed two distinct velocity stages as it raced toward Eurasia—a short but incredibly fast period at ~200 mm/yr, followed by a longer, relatively slower phase above 100 mm/yr. "We set up a plume-double subduction coupling model," Xiang says. "Our results supported the idea that the Reunion plume's push was dramatic but only sustained for a short time."

I asked about his transition from USTC to UT Austin. "The Jackson School's reputation in geodynamics is outstanding," he says. "Working with Professor Becker and having access to computational resources like TACC—it's an incredible opportunity to tackle the big questions about how our planet works."

## The Art of Computational Geodynamics

What draws someone to spend their days building computer models of processes happening hundreds of kilometers beneath our feet? For Xiang, it's the intersection of physics, geology, and computation. "I love that we can use computational methods to simulate realistic events," he explains. "But we're always constrained by observations—geology, geophysics, geochemistry. It's like solving a massive puzzle where the pieces come from different disciplines."

His current work involves setting up global mantle flow models, which requires mastering tools like CitcomS and ASPECT. "These aren't just black boxes," Xiang emphasizes. "You need to understand the physics—fluid dynamics, thermodynamics, rheology—and then translate that into code that can run on supercomputers."

I asked if his research has changed how he sees the world around him. He laughs. "Definitely. When there's an earthquake or volcanic eruption in the news, I'm immediately thinking about the mantle dynamics underneath. What's the subduction geometry? How old is the slab? It's hard to turn off that perspective."

## Beyond the Models

Outside of research, Xiang has a surprising passion: teaching. During his time at USTC, he served as a teaching assistant for Electromagnetism, working with Professor Gan Qin. "I really enjoyed helping students work through difficult concepts," he says. "There's something satisfying about seeing someone finally understand a principle they've been struggling with."

I asked if he sees teaching in his future. "Absolutely. I think being able to communicate complex ideas clearly is just as important as the research itself. What's the point of understanding Earth's deep processes if you can't share that knowledge?"

When he's not building mantle convection models or helping students with electromagnetics, Xiang enjoys exploring Austin. "Coming from Hefei to Texas has been quite a transition," he admits. "But I love the energy here—the food scene, the music, the outdoor spaces. It's a good balance to the intensity of research."

## Recognition and Future Aspirations

Xiang's undergraduate work didn't go unnoticed. He received the prestigious Guo Moruo Scholarship in 2024 and the China National Scholarship in 2023—an honor given to only the top 0.2% of students in China. "Those awards meant a lot," he says, "but they're really recognition of the mentors who guided me and the collaborative environment at USTC."

Looking ahead, Xiang is excited about the questions his PhD research might answer. "We know the mantle is heterogeneous—different viscosities, different temperatures, different compositions. But how do these deep variations manifest at the surface? Can we use GPS measurements of vertical motion to constrain what's happening 1000 kilometers down?"

He's particularly interested in incorporating more realistic physics into models. "A lot of current models make simplifying assumptions out of computational necessity. But as computing power increases, we can add more complexity—lateral viscosity variations, realistic fault systems, better rheological models. Each improvement brings us closer to understanding the real Earth."

## Advice for Future Geodynamicists

Before we wrapped up, I asked Xiang what advice he'd give to undergraduates interested in geodynamics. "Learn to code," he says immediately. "Python, MATLAB, Fortran—whatever you can. Computational skills are essential in modern geophysics."

But he quickly adds: "Don't just focus on the technical side. Take geology field trips. Read papers from different disciplines. Talk to observational geophysicists. The best models come from understanding what we're actually trying to explain."

His final thought? "Don't be intimidated by the scale of the problems. Yes, we're trying to understand processes that span millions of years and thousands of kilometers. But every big question breaks down into smaller, tractable pieces. Start with one piece, and build from there."

## The Deep Connection

As our conversation wound down, I reflected on what draws researchers like Xiang to study Earth's interior. It's not just intellectual curiosity—though there's plenty of that. It's a deep connection to understanding the fundamental forces that have shaped our planet's history and continue to drive its evolution.

"When you think about it," Xiang says, "everything we see at the surface—mountains, ocean basins, earthquakes, volcanoes—is ultimately driven by what's happening in the mantle. Understanding those deep processes helps us understand Earth as a complete system."

That's the essence of geodynamics: connecting the invisible forces beneath our feet to the world we experience every day. And researchers like Xiang are building the computational tools to make those connections clearer than ever before.
