> Three deaths completed my self-upgrade. The first death—from aspiring writer to science student—was an educational elevation. The second—from scientist to venture researcher—was a cognitive leap. Through this process, I completed a third death: I'm no longer the stereotypical science student or entrepreneur, but an entrepreneur and investor with a writer's spirit.

**TL;DR**

I grew up with two Chinese literature teachers for parents. Bored at home during middle school, I devoured over a thousand books. In high school, I won second place nationally in the World Chinese Essay Competition, but eventually decided liberal arts were too impractical and pivoted to science, scraping my way into USTC. Inspired by mentors like Academician Zheng Quanshui and Dr. Xiang Shaohua, I went from being the lowest-scoring admit from my province at 16 to being nominated for USTC's highest honor at 20. Dead set on becoming a scientist, I landed a PhD offer from UCLA EPSS (my prospective advisor there later won a Sloan Research Fellowship). But feeling that space physics was too disconnected from the real world, I turned it down and headed to UT Austin to study land surface modeling under one of the world's most senior experts.

Later, inspired by my girlfriend, I realized that academic research often has less real-world impact than building businesses and making money. I dove into economics and spent a huge chunk of my PhD trading in secondary markets. But I soon found that secondary market returns were too slow and the impact too limited. After becoming a disciple of Dr. Xiang Shaohua, I realized AI Agents are a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity for our generation. Then, under the mentorship of Kehan Dong, I discovered that entrepreneurship offers the greatest impact, growth, and returns. So, I pivoted again—diving into VC internships and launching an AI Agent startup.

### Childhood Dreams and the First Death in High School

I was born into a family of "impoverished intellectuals." My grandfather was a moderately obscure essayist, and both my parents taught Chinese literature. We had over 3,000 books and magazines crammed into our home. In middle school, banned from using a smartphone and bored out of my mind, I just read anything with words on it, tirelessly. I probably plowed through a thousand books. Back then, I dreamed of becoming a writer. I pictured myself cycling across China with a skewer rack strapped to my bike, eating barbecue and writing novels. Thanks to years of obsessive reading, I ended up winning second place nationally in the World Chinese Essay Competition during high school.

But gradually, I realized the liberal arts track wasn't for me. I hated rote memorization, and my idol was Bill Gates—I wanted the leverage to actually change the world. So, I decided to switch to science. Honestly, my science grades in high school were nothing special; I was mediocre at best, while my writing carried me. In the end, by sheer luck, I scraped into USTC during the early admission round with the absolute lowest score in my province.

My sales and leadership chops actually started building up back in 2014 when I became class monitor in middle school. My favorite book was *The Greatest Salesman in the World*. I carried it everywhere; the pages were practically falling out. I had zero hard skills back then, and nobody wanted to hear my pitches, but that book built my inner resilience.

Or, to put it more bluntly, I was "普信" (pǔxìn)—ordinary yet overly confident. I had massive path dependency because I simply hadn't experienced any spectacular failures yet. (Spoiler: failure was quietly waiting around the corner.)

### Building Self-Sustaining Flywheels: From USTC Poetry Society to "Nebula"

Even though I felt the liberal arts career path wasn't for me, I still loved literature and held onto that "poor scholar" vibe. At USTC, for some inexplicable reason, I became obsessed with writing classical Chinese poetry. I wrote feverishly, constantly posting my poems on WeChat Moments, and surprisingly, people liked them. Even Professor Liu Minghou from the Engineering School—the very guy who recruited me as the lowest-ranked student from Jiangxi—would like and encourage my posts.

That's when I noticed a gap. There were plenty of people like me who loved writing poetry, but USTC didn't have a poetry society. We had literary clubs and history associations, but no dedicated poetry group. The literary clubs focused on Western literature or classical Chinese prose, but ignored classical poetry. So, on a freezing, pouring winter night—which happened to be the birthday of a senior from the Junior Class (we often crossed paths in student government, though we didn't really know each other yet)—I started a QQ group.

From there, four co-founders and a core startup team of 20 people kicked off the "zero to one" journey of the USTC Poetry Society.

Even though it started as just a "crowdfunded" QQ group, and I didn't even have the official standing to organize events, I wanted to build it into a legitimate, university-recognized organization (the Student Nebula Poetry Society) from day one.

Because if we didn't formalize it, it would be meaningless—just another fleeting friend group lost to time. Later, I lucked out and found amazing partners like Xu Xiaohang and our faculty advisor, Professor Huang He. We survived two rounds of "funding" (official university recognition pitches), successfully secured a budget from the school management committee, and became a two-star society. Everyone worked for love, zero pay. Sometimes the guys had to pay out of pocket just to buy power strips for our events. I dealt with betrayal, and I also let people down when I dropped the ball on my own responsibilities. But overall, I was fiercely loyal. Once, at a campus fair, our booth lost power. I stormed over to confront the organizer and almost got into a fistfight. (Turns out, it wasn't his fault at all, and he was incredibly helpful in fixing the issue. Fun fact: he later won the Guo Award, completely well-deserved.)

I'm barely involved with the Nebula Poetry Society these days and rarely bring it up, but I believe it's now a self-sustaining flywheel for students on campus. (I also secretly pray it keeps thriving and that the Youth League Committee keeps funding them, haha.)

Speaking of building things from zero to one, I have to mention the place that completely altered my trajectory.

### Awakening at 01 Academy and the "Living Water" Flywheel

During the summer after my freshman year, I was bored and scrolling through group chats when I saw that the Shenzhen 01 Academy summer school was recruiting, featuring a talk by Academician Zheng Quanshui. I had relatives in Shenzhen and had visited as a kid. I figured, hey, free room and board in a great city—even if the program is grueling, it's a free trip, right? So I applied. I still remember doing my interview from a study room on the fourth floor of the West Campus library (yes, even though I lived on East Campus, I loved roaming around to study), interviewed by Senior Han Siyu and the faculty.

01 Academy spun out of Tsinghua University. I had previously applied to Tsinghua's Strong Foundation Program and was unsurprisingly rejected because my test scores and science background were too weak. But this time, I was incredibly lucky to get into 01. There, I met Academician Zheng Quanshui, Dr. Xiang Shaohua, and other incredible mentors. 01 Academy was a magical place driven by a pure mission: to discover young creators and change the world. It directly inspired my future path. More importantly, I met lifelong mentors and friends there.

At 01, I deeply internalized something Teacher Xiang always said: **"Who you learn with is more important than who you learn from."**

I still remember August 6, 2021, the last day of the first 01 summer school. Our homeroom teacher, Ren Jianxun, told us that 01 would become the Whampoa Military Academy of our generation, and we "little guys" (Teacher Xiang's nickname for us) were the founding class. He told us that when we went back to our high schools or universities, we had to keep the fire burning. We had to spark a prairie fire—not just for ourselves, but to inspire the classmates around us, telling them: "Back at 01 Academy in Shenzhen, we actually made it happen!"

Looking back at my old journals, I realized that whether you're talking about nuclear fusion, human social dynamics, building a startup, or analyzing an industry, one thing is absolutely critical: **a self-generating, perpetual-motion driving force.**

In hindsight, from Silicon Valley's hyper-efficient venture ecosystem and the PayPal Mafia's mentorship network, to the peer learning at Tsinghua's Qian Xuesen Mechanics Class and 01 Academy; from the layered architecture of blockchains to SP1's philosophy of "the strongest feeding the next generation to form a sustainable flywheel"—they are all describing the exact same thing: sustainable, living water. Carrying this obsession with "sparking a prairie fire" and "building flywheels," I went back to USTC and continued my club "entrepreneurship."

That "startup" eventually grew to 1,200 people (USTC only admits about 7,000 students a year), and over 1,000 are still active in the group today. Even though it was a non-profit, it was absolutely the training ground for everything I did later. My literary background became my ultimate **Inbound Marketing** weapon, allowing me to write compellingly, promote ideas, and build real influence.

### Determined to Become a Scientist

When I first got to USTC, I spent my days literally stopping to smell the roses. I wandered around taking photos of flowers, posting on social media, and exploring the rooftops of every building on campus. Life was pretty carefree, but reality soon hit me: with my grades and lack of focus, there was zero chance I was getting guaranteed graduate admission (保研).

"Coming to Shenzhen makes you a Shenzhener; coming to 01 means you should become a scientist." This wasn't just a fleeting burst of motivation. During the countless tea breaks, endless brainstorming sessions, and closed-door chats at that first summer school, I saw the same truth repeatedly: top-tier research is a verifiable, compounding, globally mobile career path. Being a scientist seemed so cool, so elite, so respected. For someone whose GPA wasn't exactly competitive, this path was incredibly seductive. It shook me to my core and made me seriously consider "applying for a US PhD" as an actual, executable goal. The desire to become a scientist burned stronger than ever.

I started feeling a sense of urgency. Guided by seniors like Liu Weihao, I was lucky enough to join Professor Xue Xianghui and Professor Yi Wen's research group as an undergrad. I attended domestic conferences and submitted my first first-author SCI manuscript. I also published papers under Professor Lei Jiuhou's guidance, with a lot of help from my buddy Xiaohang. At a small 01 alumni gathering, encouraged by Dean Wu Heng'an of the Engineering School, I even started to glimpse the possibility of winning the prestigious Guo Award.

As I mentioned, I almost didn't make the cut for grad school. But thanks to 01, Dr. Xiang Shaohua's guidance, the immense patience of Professors Xue and Yi at USTC, and the help of my seniors, I genuinely fell in love with research and the idea of a US PhD. Plotting meteor data in MATLAB suddenly felt like the most beautiful thing in the world—even cooler than building slick personal websites in HTML.

I was incredibly fortunate that Professor Xue introduced me to Professor Liu for a research exchange. After jumping through endless hoops, I got my visa; 01 and USTC provided the funding I needed to survive.

It's funny how dramatic life is. In 2020, I was rejected by Tsinghua's Strong Foundation Program. By 2023, I was invited back to Tsinghua as a student representative of 01 Academy, sharing my innovation story with educators from across the country at a top talent symposium. On May 28, 2023, standing on Tsinghua's campus, I wrote a poem: "Promised to be first-rate among people, gathered in Pengcheng and set sail again. Where is the long tether of ten thousand miles? Laughing at the deep blue small globe."

Two months later, I left campus and took my first flight across the globe to America. Under Professor Liu's hands-on mentorship and the crushing pressure of PhD application deadlines, I became truly obsessed with research.

In Boulder—a stunning little mountain town—I spent entire nights in the office, using ChatGPT to debug my hand-typed code, and reading my PhD application essays out loud over and over. I meticulously revised manuscripts, crashing on the office floor when I was too exhausted to keep my eyes open, and waking up at dawn. Seeing the red sunrise hit the white snow while the birds chirped, I knew spring was coming.

I wasn't going to get rejected everywhere. Back in China, during the 01 winter school, I was taking a break on the set of a podcast with heavyweights like Han and Qiu when I got my first offer from Boulder. I am deeply grateful to Professors Wang and Liu from Earth and Space Sciences, and my homeroom teacher Dai. I even had the insane luck of becoming one of two publicly announced nominees for the Guo Award, alongside my good friend He Xiang (who applied for Category A). We had somehow managed to win first prize together for an electromagnetics paper. I was even honored to receive recommendation letters from Academician Zheng for both the Guo Award and my PhD applications.

I applied to 12 schools and got 4 PhD offers, including one from UCLA EPSS (huge thanks to Professor Cao for believing in me; he later won the Sloan Research Fellowship). But I felt that space physics was too disconnected from everyday human life, and that land surface modeling was more practical. So, I turned down UCLA and headed to UT Austin to study under one of the world's most senior experts in the field.

An interesting quirk about my path dependency: **I absolutely despise classes.** Ever since high school, I've had a deep aversion to any course that requires sitting still for 40+ minutes and ends with a formalized exam. Honestly, I think almost all university classes after freshman year are completely useless (except for assigning a GPA). Looking back at my time at USTC: the project that got me nominated for the Guo Award at 20 wasn't taught in a lecture. I didn't learn how to publish a first-author SCI paper in class. Hackathon skills weren't on the syllabus. Nobody taught me how to apply for funding in a seminar. Even the professors who influenced me the most, my closest friends, and my girlfriend—I met almost none of them in a classroom.

The current education system was designed decades, if not centuries, ago. It completely ignores how human beings actually learn. I firmly believe that in the AI era, the reality that "people no longer need classes" will become painfully obvious.

### "Think About Others, Use Your Brain"

During my freshman year, in a Wuhan University calligraphy competition group chat, I met a girl who would eventually become my girlfriend. But it took three years for us to truly reconnect. When we did, it felt like reuniting with an old friend after a century apart, yet also like looking in a mirror. We both regretted not getting together sooner.

During that period, I got arrogant. As a guy at USTC (which is notoriously male-dominated), I had actually managed to find a girlfriend. I thought I was invincible. I thought everyone loved me. I was wrong. I was so high on my own success narrative that I forgot I was actually a nobody—just a pig catching a lucky updraft.

In Suzhou, breathing in the terrible smog, I heard the most valuable piece of advice I've received in years. It still echoes in my head: "Think about others, use your brain." An old friend from 01 told me that.

After that, I moved to UT Austin, and she went to Tsinghua for her PhD. (Because of that, I ended up living in Haidian for nearly six months total, during which I interned at three different startups and became friends with all three founders.)

My girlfriend is one of the most patient and forgiving people I've ever met. Looking back, I was incredibly incompetent a year ago, and she put up with way too much of my nonsense. She pushed me to figure out how to make money, because without it, we couldn't fund any of our dreams. But I made excuses. I told her coding was too hard, that I couldn't do it—which is ironic, considering I built a personal website sophomore year and minored in CS before eventually dropping it.

### The Third Death: Facing Death, Entering AI

A year after leaving the mountains of Boulder, I crossed the ocean again, this time landing in the small city of Austin to start my PhD at UT Texas. My first year was a nightmare. My sleep was wrecked, my focus was shot, and my anxiety was through the roof. One day, I literally thought I was dying—I was convulsing on my bed. My saint of a roommate called 911. I was hauled off in an ambulance and ended up in the ER twice.

Staring death in the face strips away all your armor. Everyone is fragile.

I started desperately seeking medical help. I brewed traditional Chinese medicine in my office, and the smell was so overpowering that the faculty reported me to the department. Because I was naive and completely buried in my research, my roommates and I got scammed on our lease, losing nearly a month's salary. Then, on a totally ordinary afternoon, right after drinking my bitter medicine, I got the news that a relative I deeply respected had passed away.

I spiraled. I started studying the *I Ching* (Book of Changes), built a divination website to predict my luck, and completely lost interest in my research. My academic output flatlined.

I flew back to China for medical treatment, only to discover that the "doctor" I had been revering was a total quack. On my way home from the clinic, out of sheer naive kindness, I got conned by a scammer pretending to be a stranded corporate executive who had lost his wallet. He drained the little cash I had left.

When my naive goodwill violently collided with reality, leaving me bruised and bleeding on the edge of life and death, I was forced to re-evaluate the world and human nature. Was academia really the optimal path? Why hadn't any of my teachers prepared me for the real world?

I couldn't find the answers internally, so I started throwing rocks to find a path forward.

I remembered what Teacher Zhang Wenzeng had told me a year prior: you have to speak up more, to express yourself as freely as Wang Xu does. Speaking your mind is a defense mechanism against depression and anxiety.

So, I started shitposting online. Despite almost everyone mocking me at first, I built an audience of 9,000 followers. Meanwhile, I kept getting medical checkups, fixed my sleep schedule, and realized a hilarious truth: I was actually healthier than 99% of the population. There was absolutely nothing physically wrong with me.

So I threw the damn medicine in the trash and blocked the charlatans.

Here's a funny story. Through a disciple of an academician from the Seventh Academy, I met Brother Yu Zaishui. Inspired by him, I asked a professor for a referral for a quantitative finance internship. I didn't get the job, but the interviewer gave me a golden piece of advice: "If you want to do quant, you need to actually make some trades. Even paper trading proves you actually care about the space." That pushed me down the rabbit hole of a world I knew nothing about: the secondary markets (or, as normal people call it, trading stocks).

During my breaks, I poured massive amounts of time into the markets. I took losses, even blew up an account, but it forged my mental resilience. Most importantly, I realized I was no longer just a fisherman in a quiet reservoir; I was a helmsman on the open ocean. I finally earned the right to love risk and volatility.

I also picked up an old hobby: rock climbing. I once posted this on WeChat:

> "Life is exactly like rock climbing. Every move upward is a trade-off. When your forearms are completely pumped and failing, your mind still screams at you to keep going up.
>
> Do you dyno for the next hold? You have zero strength left—if you miss, you take a massive whip and have to rest for ages.
>
> Do you drop down, rest on the mats, memorize the beta, and start over? Sure, but by the time you're ready, the moment might have passed. A thousand other people have already flashed the route while you're still chalking up at the start.
>
> The worst part is, you can't expect to have the same explosive motivation on your fifth attempt as you did when you first walked into the gym today. You're a human, not a machine.
>
> But the good news? You're young. You have time. You have enough runway to fail. Keep climbing—maybe after a quick rest and studying the beta, you'll flash two harder grades in a row next time."

I also started swimming to get my physical strength back.

By this point, I ran the numbers and realized that relying purely on secondary market trading meant slow asset growth and zero personal leverage. If you only look at the "growth curve," academic citations actually compound faster in the early days, almost exponentially. The fatal flaw? The feedback loop with the real world is agonizingly long, and you have zero control over the outcome. It completely clashed with the tempo I craved.

Because of this mismatch, I adopted Taleb's barbell strategy. While keeping my main focus on my PhD research, I started hunting for a path that offered rapid iteration and direct value creation.

As I mentioned earlier, I met my closest friends at 01 Academy. Even reconnecting with my girlfriend happened because I re-gifted her a beautiful piece of 01 merch.

At 01, the floor right below us was the Shenzhen Innovation Academy. One of my summer school roommates was a top student from there who had come to 01 purely to farm networking connections.

My first industry internship was with that old 01 friend, working in BioTech. Honestly, I was completely useless—I probably caused more problems than I solved. But that failure taught me exactly what a top-tier intern actually looks like. Later, during the summer of 2025 in Beijing, I juggled three internships. I went from vibe-coding a new project every single day, to being hired by study-abroad agencies to build custom RAG knowledge bases, to developing AI essay-polishing systems. I met Brother Boj and built an AI Agent evaluation system that could actually make phone calls. I even took my best friend from 01 on a tour of one of China's top university incubators at Tsinghua.

My friend told me he regularly pulled all-nighters, stressed out of his mind over his startup. But he also knew that if the company hit a major milestone, the financial upside would be life-changing. He might be renting a cramped apartment in Hefei now, but soon enough, he'd be able to buy a house in cash.

I traveled to Shanghai to hang out with USTC professors and students building AI, with kids from the Innovation Academy, and with founders from the Zhongguancun and Hetao academies.

These people iterate at lightspeed, whether they are interns or founders. They take massive risks—one wrong move and they're bankrupt.

But their execution speed is terrifying. A bug found in the afternoon is patched and shipped by 3 AM. Every time I talked to them, I could feel their raw, explosive, upward momentum.

If you can't beat them, join them. I started grinding at hackathons. I teamed up with classmates, won second place at a university competition, and walked away with a hundred bucks—enough for a few good meals. I competed solo and started shipping open-source projects on a regular cadence.

At first, nobody cared. I was still shitposting, and some alumni kindly pulled me aside to tell me I was spamming the group chats, wasting everyone's time, and that I should be sending red envelopes (money) to compensate people for the noise.

I took the hint. I stopped spamming and started sharing genuinely high-signal content. Over time, my taste for quality information sharpened dramatically. I hit 4,000 GitHub commits this year, my repo stars are climbing, and whenever I sit down to work, I have four or five AI Agents running in the background doing the heavy lifting. Call it fake-it-till-you-make-it or just bragging, but I slowly brute-forced my way into the AI circle.

At the same time, the math of academia started looking worse and worse. The ROI on being a scientist is terrible. Theoretical breakthroughs rarely translate into real-world value. Why did Fei-Fei Li leave to raise billions for a vision model startup? Why is Yann LeCun the Chief Scientist at Meta? Why does every single CS professor at Stanford have a startup on the side? Why? Because in this era, founders are the most efficient, highest-leverage creators on the planet.

I have a great friend from the USTC x 01 network, Yu Chenglin. We talk constantly, bouncing ideas off each other. One random day, he sent me an article written by an angel investor: "How to Spot a 20-Year-Old Worth Investing In."

Coincidentally, this investor was doing a US tour, giving talks to Chinese students.

Coincidentally, his next stop was Austin.

His name was Kehan Dong.

That night, a huge group of us packed into his lecture.

The next day, I had the incredible luck of grabbing coffee with him and Chonghao, a brilliant young founder. We talked about reality, ideals, our heroes, and what the next few decades of human history are going to look like.

That conversation flipped a switch in my brain. I realized with absolute clarity that entrepreneurship offers the deepest impact, the fastest growth, and the most asymmetric returns of any career path.

I immediately asked to become his disciple, desperate to learn the DNA of top-tier founders.

I can't share all the specifics of the projects and resources we discussed, but they all pointed to the same three brutal truths: First, can you validate the technology-to-value loop in *weeks*, not *years*? Second, is the team psychotic enough to iterate relentlessly under crushing pressure? Third, is the cost of failure capped while the upside is virtually infinite? Those three criteria pushed me over the edge. I stopped being a spectator and finally stepped into the arena.

Recently, I competed in a Google Gemini hackathon. By pure coincidence, I found out my teammate had been quietly building something massive in the background. Naturally, I accepted his invitation and joined as the Technical Co-founder of this AI startup. Now, I'm operating with an investor's macro thesis and a founder's execution speed, finally getting my hands dirty in the venture ecosystem.

### From Pain Point to Product: The Birth of Tacit AI

I originally dove into Earth system science because I wanted to tackle the massive, existential threat of climate change. But during the transition from my undergrad at USTC to my PhD at UT Austin—and especially while grinding at NSF NCAR—I got hit with a brutal reality check: the biggest bottleneck in scientific research isn't a lack of compute power. It's our completely broken knowledge infrastructure.

Earth system modeling relies on terrifyingly complex codebases and hyper-specific experimental designs. But getting any of it to actually work depends entirely on "tacit knowledge"—the dark matter of research. How do you tune this specific parameter? How do you debug this 20-year-old legacy Fortran script? Why did some postdoc choose this bizarre configuration five years ago? None of it is documented. The second a key collaborator graduates or leaves for industry, all that context vanishes into thin air. Projects grind to a halt, and the compounding interest of scientific progress is completely wiped out.

I quickly realized I wasn't the only one suffering through this. At a hackathon, I met Yuxuan Cao. Even though we came from totally different academic worlds—geosciences and physics—we instantly bonded over the exact same nightmare: undocumented workflows and evaporating institutional knowledge. We decided to stop bitching about the problem and actually write some code to fix it.

Acting as the technical builder from Day 0, I teamed up with Yuxuan to hack together our very first prototype, Atomize. We just wanted to prove a hypothesis: could we actually capture and operationalize the "dark knowledge" that researchers are so desperate for?

The prototype worked, and suddenly, things started moving very fast. Yuxuan introduced me to our two business co-founders, and they formally asked me to join the founding team. For me, it was a no-brainer. During my VC internship working under a founding member of Y Combinator China, I learned to view academic bottlenecks not as personal failings, but as systemic infrastructure failures. More importantly, I had seen up close how elite founders operate—how they move violently fast, build scalable systems, and engineer viral growth. My gut told me this team had the exact right DNA to take our hackathon toy and turn it into a company that could actually change the industry. So, I went all in.

Today, that scrappy prototype has evolved into Tacit AI. We are building the AI infrastructure required to help researchers capture tacit knowledge, eliminate friction, and actually leverage human experience instead of losing it. We aren't just building another generic project management SaaS; we are building an operating system designed to capture the "why" and "how" behind every single decision made in a lab. We want complex research to move forward without constantly dropping context.

As the Technical Co-founder, I am incredibly proud of how genuinely useful our product has already become. I feel this pain point in my bones because I live the miserable reality of modern scientific workflows every single day. If we can build a better infrastructure to capture and operationalize human expertise, we can dramatically accelerate the entire pace of scientific discovery.

That is exactly why I'm building Tacit AI—to build the tool I desperately needed to survive my PhD, and to create the infrastructure that every researcher actually deserves.

### Negation of Negation: An Antifragile Life

Three deaths completed my self-upgrade. The first death—killing the aspiring writer to become a science student—was an educational leveling-up. The second death—killing the pure scientist to become a venture researcher—was a massive cognitive leap. And through all of this, I completed my third death: I am no longer the stereotypical socially awkward science nerd, nor am I a generic tech bro. I am an entrepreneur and investor who still bleeds with the spirit of a writer.

I've kept a journal for 20 years, forcing myself to output in order to drive my input. That gave me my superpower: Inbound Marketing. Grinding through scientific research gave me a rock-solid mathematical foundation, allowing me to instantly spot who's full of shit and who actually has hard tech. My VC internships gave me the lens of capital. These three deaths—like the Marxist concept of the "negation of the negation," a critique of a critique—have forged me into something antifragile. I'm an unkillable cockroach.

Right now, my life is a chaotic, exhausting grind: juggling a PhD, building a startup, investing, and figuring out how to actually make money. The old Wu Koutian, the kid who blindly followed the conventional track, is dead. But that death makes me feel like being alive is incredibly valuable. My ultimate goal is to build phenomenal AI assistants, tackle the massive philosophical questions of human-AI collaboration, and use AI to give everyone massive leverage in their lives. I want to be a relentless growth iterator—someone who deeply understands hard tech, wields Inbound Marketing like a weapon, and personally engineers the compounding interest of the future. I'm going to build the next self-sustaining flywheel.
