My verdict on Air Canada after flying from Singapore to Austin through Vancouver is simple: **what a mess.**

The journey was not uniformly bad. It felt more like a randomized experiment. On the fourteen-hour trans-Pacific leg, I unexpectedly had an entire row to myself—but spent the flight next to a lavatory odor. The connecting flight then involved a go-around, a towing delay, and an ugly recent on-time sample. Yet after eating Pad Thai at YVR, I crashed from the carbohydrates and slept through nearly the entire flight.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

## AC20: I had seats 30A, 30B, and 30C to myself

On July 19, 2026, I took Air Canada AC20 from Singapore to Vancouver, a flight of roughly fourteen hours.

After boarding, I discovered that I was the only passenger in row 30's A, B, and C seats. This is close to the best random outcome an economy passenger can get: room to lie sideways, no fight over armrests, and no need to keep moving for seatmates.

Unfortunately, I am 182 cm tall. Three economy seats still were not long enough for me to lie flat. I had to bend my legs, sleep diagonally, and keep changing positions. It was far better than sitting upright for fourteen hours, but it was not a bed.

The real problem was row 30's proximity to the lavatory.

This was not an occasional smell when someone opened the door. For long stretches, the lavatory odor persisted. I had three seats, but it felt as if I had slept outside a restroom for fourteen hours. The luck of extra space was steadily cancelled by the cabin air.

That contradiction defined AC20:

- I won the space lottery.
- At 182 cm, I still could not lie flat.
- I was surrounded by lavatory odor.
- I nevertheless slept better than I would have in a full economy row.

A long-haul experience depends on more than whether adjacent seats are empty. Seat location, ventilation, cleaning frequency, and odor control determine whether passengers can rest. Two empty seats were luck; persistent lavatory odor was a cabin-operations problem.

## At YVR, the Air Canada problems kept stacking up

After AC20 reached Vancouver, I connected to AC1762 for Austin.

When I checked AC1762 in Umetrip, its 30-day display initially showed three on-time flights among five samples. After today's flight was added, it became three among six—moving the small displayed sample from 60% to 50%.

This is not enough evidence to estimate the route's long-run performance. Six observations are too few, and Umetrip's display may not match an airline or regulator's official on-time metric. But for a passenger waiting that day, the signal was direct: **this flight had not looked reliable recently, and mine added another delay.**

Air Canada sent this message:

> Your flight AC1762 to Austin is delayed because we need more time to tow the aircraft. It will now depart at 14:20.

The flight had been scheduled for around 1 p.m. It was now pushed to 2:20 p.m. The stated reason was not weather or air traffic control, but the need for more time to tow the aircraft. From a passenger's perspective, this is a uniquely deflating explanation: the aircraft exists, yet the operation still needs more time to move it where it belongs.

There was also a go-around that day. A go-around is a standard safety procedure. If the runway, weather, aircraft spacing, or approach is not safe, climbing away is the correct decision. I would never want pilots to force a landing for punctuality, and I cannot determine the specific cause from a passenger's viewpoint.

Still, the travel experience combined the go-around, ground turnaround, towing, and delay into a distinctly Air Canada storyline: **the safety decision can be right while the ground operation remains a mess.**

## Pad Thai ended my ability to complain

During the YVR connection, I ate Pad Thai. The carbohydrate load, combined with trans-Pacific fatigue, put me into an immediate food coma.

So despite AC1762's delay and everything that preceded it, I slept through nearly the entire flight. In a strange sense, this became the journey's most effective service recovery. Air Canada did not repair the experience; Pad Thai simply switched off my consciousness.

AC1762 therefore produced a peculiar result:

- Operationally, it reinforced my poor impression of Air Canada.
- Physically, I was too tired to stay angry.
- Experientially, I slept through the segment instead of enduring it awake.
- In the final score, the airline did not win. Pad Thai did.

## Not a disaster, but a chain of avoidable friction

I completed the SIN → YVR → AUS journey. The go-around reflected a safety-first decision, and the delay did not destroy the trip. The empty row on AC20 even gave me unusually good economy-class sleep.

But an airline should not be judged only by whether passengers eventually arrive. We should also ask:

- Can the cabin control odor near the lavatories?
- Is the flight turnaround stable?
- Are aircraft, gates, tow crews, and equipment coordinated on time?
- Do delay notices provide explanations passengers can understand?
- Do small failures keep stacking up until they drain passengers' energy and trust?

No single issue on this trip was a disaster. The problem was the chain: fourteen hours of lavatory odor, a confusing YVR transfer, a weak recent on-time sample, a go-around, towing, and delay.

In the end, luck gave me a row, Pad Thai carried me through the next flight, and I carried myself through the journey. Air Canada completed the transportation. It did not deliver an experience I would trust.
