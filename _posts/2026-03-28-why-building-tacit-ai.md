Why I'm Building Tacit AI
**By Koutian Wu**

I came into Earth system science because I wanted to tackle some of the most pressing climate and hydrology challenges of our time. But as I transitioned from my undergraduate studies at USTC to my PhD research at UT Austin, I realized that one of the biggest barriers to scientific progress isn't a lack of computing power or ambition. It comes from broken knowledge infrastructure: too much expertise living exclusively in researchers' heads, and complex modeling efforts depending entirely on a few individuals to keep them running.

I saw this clearly during my time as a visiting scholar at NSF NCAR. Earth system modeling—like the work I do with the Noah-MP model—involves massive datasets, intricate codebases, and simultaneous experiments with collaborators spanning multiple time zones. In the lab, I quickly learned that the formal documentation only scratches the surface. The real work relies entirely on tacit knowledge: how to tune specific model parameters, how to debug legacy code, or the unwritten history of why a certain configuration was chosen years ago. 

When a key collaborator or senior postdoc leaves, that context disappears with them. I watched projects spin into chaos and stall for months simply because the "dark knowledge" of how to actually run the experiments was gone. We weren't just losing time; we were losing compounding scientific progress. 

My perspective on how to solve this shifted during my VC internship with a founding member of Y Combinator China. I learned to look at these academic bottlenecks as a systems failure. The most transformative companies don't just build faster tools; they build infrastructure that captures how people actually work, think, and create together. I realized that science desperately needs this kind of system.

That is why I am building Tacit AI. I want to build AI infrastructure that helps researchers preserve tacit knowledge, reduce friction, and operationalize human experience. We aren't building just another project management app. We are building a system that captures the "why" and "how" behind the decisions made in the lab, so complex research can move forward without losing context every time someone graduates or changes roles.

My advantage is that I understand this problem from the inside. As a geosciences PhD student, I live the reality of modern scientific work: undocumented workflows, fragmented data, and constant dependence on unwritten knowledge. If we can build better infrastructure to capture and operationalize this expertise, we can dramatically accelerate the pace of discovery. That is why I'm building Tacit AI—to create the operating system for knowledge that every researcher deserves.
