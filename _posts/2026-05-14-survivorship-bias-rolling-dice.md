I've been watching too many people who got lucky rolling a six six times in a row, standing on stage lecturing us about their dice-rolling strategies.

> Author: [Koutian Wu](https://www.linkedin.com/in/ktwu01/); [GitHub: ktwu01](https://github.com/ktwu01/)

On January 27th of this year, I scribbled a random thought into my diary.

That thought was exactly the scene described in the title. Honestly, it just makes you sigh.

We all know the drill. Open any platform, and you will see founders, tech moguls, and newly minted unicorns standing under the spotlight, generously sharing their 「success stories」. They tell you how visionary that one specific decision was, how relentlessly their team worked, and how sharp their business intuition was.

Listen to it long enough, and you start believing that if you just copy their exact steps, you can do it too.

But frankly speaking, if you look closely, a lot of what we call success is simply a probability game. They were just incredibly lucky to roll a six, six times in a row, right in the middle of a perfect macroeconomic cycle.

Of course, I am not literally talking about someone sitting there with a physical die. The six-sixes metaphor is my way of describing how much luck sits inside public success. A person who eventually becomes famous often has to pass through many probabilistic gates: the era they were born into, the industry they entered, the cycle they caught, the cofounders they met, whether the market happened to be ready when the product launched, whether the fundraising window happened to be open, and whether a key customer, investor, policy shift, or mentor appeared at exactly the right time.

Each of those things can be explained afterward as skill. But when you stack them together, you start seeing a huge amount of randomness. Rolling six sixes in a row is a metaphor for hitting several low-probability moments in sequence.

I am not saying their hard work is worthless. I am saying the massive weight of luck is conveniently ignored.

The tricky part is that winners naturally get narrative power. When they look back, randomness becomes inevitability. A survivor sample becomes a playbook. 「I happened to still be at that table」 turns into 「I understood the rules of the table all along.」 If the audience is not careful, someone else's outcome starts looking like a manual.

This realization really messes with your head for a second. If it is all just luck, why bother trying anything at all?

I thought about this for a while, and the answer is actually quite liberating. If you accept that success is heavily skewed by probability, the solution becomes brutally simple.

For young people, the goal is not to study 「how to roll a six」. That is pure mysticism. You can not learn that.

What you should do instead is just roll the dice. 

If you roll it enough times, the law of large numbers guarantees you will eventually hit a six. It is just math.

But here is the catch. There is one massive prerequisite.

「Make sure you do not die.」

That is the absolute core of the game. It is very close to the simple logic Buffett keeps coming back to: make sure you are still at the table. If you are still at the table, you still have another hand to play. Once you are forced out, the next opportunity no longer matters to you, no matter how obvious it looks in hindsight.

A lot of people roll the dice by betting their entire life savings, taking on massive leverage, and quitting their jobs to go all in. They roll a two on their first try, go completely bankrupt, and permanently lose their seat at the table. They never get to roll again.

Think about it. If the cost of your trials is extremely low, a failure just means dusting yourself off and trying again tomorrow. You get to play the game infinitely. Keep your day job to pay the bills, and hack on your side project at night. Validate your ideas with almost zero capital. That is what it means to stay alive.

Survival gives you infinite ammo.

So the skill worth training is not some mystical technique for rolling a six. It is risk control. Control your bet size. Control the cost of failure. Control your cash flow. Control your emotions. Control the temptation to push every future chip onto one opportunity that merely looks obvious today. For young people especially, the real advantage is not always better judgment. It is more time, fewer obligations, and a faster ability to adjust. But only if you do not burn all of those advantages on one irreversible bet.

I always feel that the most impressive entrepreneurs are not the ones who hit a three-pointer on their first try. They are the ones who shot ten thousand times and somehow managed to not get kicked off the court.

Just stay at the table. Keep flipping the coin. Eventually, you will get a streak of heads.

And then what?

And then, you can put on a nice suit, walk onto that spotlighted stage, and calmly share your 「dice-rolling experience」 with the younger generation.
