Today I stood before the Alamo chapel in San Antonio. This seemingly peaceful building holds one of the most brutal and legendary chapters in Texas and American history.

## Spanish Mission Origins (1718-1793)

This place wasn't originally called the Alamo. It was **Mission San Antonio de Valero**, established by Spanish colonizers to convert local Native Americans to Catholicism and consolidate Spanish control on the frontier.

The iconic "church" with its distinctive curved parapet that we see today was actually just a small part of a much larger mission complex.

## How "Alamo" Got Its Name

In the early 19th century, a Spanish cavalry unit from Alamo de Parras, Mexico, was stationed here. Combined with the cottonwood trees (*álamo* in Spanish) that grew abundantly in the area, people began calling this place "the Alamo."

## 1836: Thirteen Days of Resistance

This is the Alamo's most famous moment. In 1836, Texians rebelled against Mexican President Santa Anna's dictatorship, seeking independence.

**The Siege Begins**: On February 23, 1836, Mexican forces arrived in San Antonio. Approximately 189 to 250 defenders retreated into the Alamo, including the famous:
- William B. Travis
- James Bowie
- The legendary Davy Crockett

**Thirteen Days**: Against overwhelming odds, they held out for 13 days. Travis wrote his famous letter pledging "Victory or Death."

**The Final Fall**: In the early morning of March 6, Mexican forces launched their final assault and breached the defenses. Nearly all the Texian defenders died in battle.

## "Remember the Alamo!"

Though the Alamo fell, this brutal sacrifice became a rallying cry for the Texas army. Six weeks later, Sam Houston led Texian forces shouting **"Remember the Alamo!"** at the Battle of San Jacinto, defeating the Mexican army and ultimately winning Texas independence.

## The Building's Evolution

Interestingly, the iconic curved parapet "hump" at the top of the church was actually added in 1850 when the U.S. Army took over and repaired the roof. During the 1836 battle, it didn't have that distinctive "forehead."
